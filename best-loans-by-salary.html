Popular Questions About Loans in Kenya

What documents are required to apply for a bank loan or mobile loan in Kenya?

Most banks, SACCOs, and licensed mobile lenders in Kenya will require the following documents before approving your loan:

  • Valid Kenyan National ID
  • KRA PIN certificate
  • Recent payslips or proof of income (last 3 months)
  • Bank statements or M-PESA statements
  • Completed loan application form

Additional documents may be required depending on the type of loan, loan amount, and the lender’s eligibility criteria.

How to get a personal loan in Kenya with a low salary?

If your salary is below Ksh 20,000, you can still access microloans and low-limit personal loans from mobile lenders, SACCOs, or bank salary advance products. Key tips include:

  • Maintain a positive CRB record
  • Borrow smaller amounts and repay on time to increase your loan limit
  • Consider group lending or guarantor-backed SACCO loans

Low-salary loans often have shorter repayment periods and smaller amounts, but can be a stepping stone to higher loan limits.

Current interest rates for loans in Kenya (2025)

Loan interest rates in Kenya vary widely depending on the lender and product type:

  • Mobile loans: 7% – 15% per month
  • Bank personal loans: 13% – 16% per annum (plus processing fees)
  • SACCO loans: 1% – 1.5% per month on reducing balance

Always check the APR (Annual Percentage Rate) to understand the true cost of borrowing.

Can I get a loan in Kenya if I am blacklisted on CRB?

Most commercial banks and SACCOs require a clean CRB report. However, some licensed microfinance institutions and select mobile lenders may offer loans to blacklisted customers, often at higher interest rates or with collateral requirements.

It is advisable to clear your CRB status to access better interest rates and higher loan limits.

Top tips for fast loan approval in Kenya
  • Ensure your ID and KRA PIN are valid and up to date
  • Have at least 3 months’ salary proof or M-PESA transaction history
  • Maintain a consistent income deposit pattern in your account
  • Apply for an amount within your affordability range
  • Keep your CRB record clean for better approval chances

Everything You Need to Know About Salary-Based Loans in Kenya

What Are Salary-Based Loans?

Salary-based loans are credit facilities offered to employed individuals whose income is verifiable. The loan amount and eligibility are primarily determined by your monthly salary. These loans include salary advances, check-off loans, unsecured personal loans, and tailored packages from banks, SACCOs, and mobile lenders.

Repayments are usually deducted directly from your salary or paid monthly from your salary account. Most providers in Kenya require you to have a salaried job for at least 3–6 months and an active salary account.

  • KCB Salary Advance: Up to Ksh 100,000; repay in 1–6 months
  • Equity Bank Personal Loan: Flexible repayment up to 60 months
  • Fin Kenya: Salary-based mobile loans up to Ksh 200,000
  • Co-op Bank MCo-opCash: Instant salary advances for account holders
  • Stima SACCO Loans: Low interest and check-off terms for salaried members

All loans are subject to eligibility, CRB status, and employer validation (if required).

Benefits of Salary-Based Loans
  • Fast disbursement – some loans available within hours
  • No collateral required in most cases
  • Flexible repayment aligned with payday cycles
  • Improves credit history if paid on time

Whether you're handling emergencies or consolidating debt, salary-linked loans are a useful tool if managed responsibly.

Risks & Fees to Consider
  • Interest rates can range from 7% to 15% per month (or more)
  • Excise duty (20%) often applies on fees
  • Late repayment penalties or CRB blacklisting possible
  • Repeated use can reduce take-home salary and cause debt traps

Always read terms carefully and borrow only what you can repay comfortably.

Who Qualifies for a Salary-Based Loan?
  • Employed Kenyans earning a regular monthly income (Ksh 10,000 and above)
  • Minimum 3 months employment history (for banks)
  • Clean or fair CRB score
  • Salary paid to a known commercial bank or mobile money account
